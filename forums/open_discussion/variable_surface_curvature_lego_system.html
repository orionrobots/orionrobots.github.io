Imagine being able to make variable surface curvatures such as spheres, tubes, torus/doughnuts, terraced planes and dish structures etc out of only two simple components. One component is a flat hexagonal plate and the other is a flat square plate
The novelty is, is that the hexagonal plates have hinges on all six edges whilst the squares only need hinges on their two opposed edges. The two parts can click into one another allowing them to hinge.
Basically you make a flexible space matrix by joining, two parallel planes of hexagons together, in a particular manner, using the vertically orientated squares as the walls.
The hex plates would have lego buttons moulded into their surface area. Upon completion of this matrix, the modeller is then free to build upon it.
By the addition of clickability into the hinge design one can build a surface area which could be incrementally morphed from one curvature into another.

The question is-would you welcome such a concept??

I''d suggest a couple of places.
First - the search "Lego Dimensions", shows many images with various aspects for them - handy to know. I''ve used it plenty of times, and because different images have different ones, you''ll need to combine a few to get the bits you want (Lego actually has a number of attachment systems - stud/hole, pin/hole, round/clip (inner stud) and so on.

The Second thing is too look at "Bitbeam" - a gridbeam clone made with Lego studless construction in mind - Bitbeam is a version of grid beam - open source construction modular stuff, made with simple laser cut wood, designed to interoperate with Lego stud less beam construction styles. http://bitbeam.org/

Finally - there will be nothing more accurate than grabbing a couple of Lego pins (both friction and smooth), some Lego beams and using a digital calliper to take your own measurements. You may need to make a few mock up bits to get the stuff fitting together nicely.
