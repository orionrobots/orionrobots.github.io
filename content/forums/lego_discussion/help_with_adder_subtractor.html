' date: Fri 26 of May, 2006 00:12 BST - author: orion content: '

Hi Legofan,

Actually, what you are seeing is what I would expect. The motor A should be straight forward or back, and if you actually set motor B to brake (not float), then the drive itself should iron out some of the weight imbalance, although veer due to friction (or slip) cant be helped by the drive, and I use six wheels with fat tyres to try and deal with that. Motor A is always the forward/reverse motor. Now motor B is a bit different.
If you consider the outputs L and R, then (and this might be the other way around depending on your design), output L is A + B, and output R is A - B. This means that when you brake motor A, and power motor B, the wheels are expected to turn in opposite directions.

Now one problem I have encountered with any Skid Steering robot is that of wheel drag - you may want to look at how close together the sides are, and how long the vehicle is. If it is too long and thing, the wheels will be creating lateral (sideways to their running) friction - which is what may make it appear to be struggling against a heavy load on a high friction surface. At this point, you need one of two things - to change the robots geometry so the wheels are closer to a square than a rectangle, or to use holonomic wheels, which cannot be bought from Lego, but can be constructed with a bit of ingenuity.

One thing I have been looking at at the moment is a nice way to double up the A and B inputs to the system. My solution has been to use another diff on each input, feeding two motors into a diff, and taking the diff output as an input into the adder subtractor inputs. This will compensate for the fact that in a normal skid steer bot, two motors are hauling the load and in this it is down to one to struggle along.

Anyway, I hope this helps you out Legofan,

Danny
