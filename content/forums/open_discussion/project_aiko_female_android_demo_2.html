Hi Sam,

Good to hear that. Keep me posted on your efforts. If you have a site, let me know, and we will happily link to it. Otherwise, I can set you up a blog here for your robot stuff if you would like.

Using the net for intelligence has pros and cons. Perhaps the biggest pros are that it is easy to upgrade, and is not limited to the size needed to fit it into the robot.

However the cons are somewhat awkward. First there is the lag time, a robot must first send sensory data, then process and then send it back. This could make a conversation very slow. The lag could make it unbearable to interact with the bot. The other con is that the robot then has a very limited sphere of operation - within range and without obstructions of the transmitter. Using the cell phone, or citywide wireless meshes where they exist can deal with the second problem but not the first.

Also a shared network may mean the bandwidth needed to do any kind of image or speech processing interactively is simply not available, or meeting a lot of contention.

I suspect that offboard intelligence is fine for a robot expected to operate in a well defined area, and not interact with people, but I am not so sure of it being good for personal relations. Perhaps it would serve well for a robotic receptionist, meant to stay in basically one place, and therefore able to have a pretty high bandwidth dedicated wireless band.

Bear in mind that ALICE and other AI will now run quite comfortably on small scale devices. I remember running an early ALICE bot on a DOS based machine in the 90''s. Most phones and PDA''s will run it. Speech processing can be done on a single chip, and with things like the CMU cam or similar, camera processing can also be done on other chips, with the main CPU simply receiving high level versions of things at those inputs. Wireless is probably best used for back ups and software/firmware updates, as well as occasionally dipping into expert systems on the net for facts.

Cheers,
Danny

Thanks Le,

Have you heard of the PINO platform? It is a project to provide a platform for android like robotics. It only describes the servos and mechanical structure, and does not at all cover the programming or other aspects, but it is open source.

I think like you say, with enough interested people working on a platform like this, it will soon evolve and become more complex, but the barriers to overcome are that this is very expensive to get into and parts are hard to source. My focus on small scale prototyping and manufacturing in my own current work (which I am yet to document properly on the site) is to try and really seriously lower this barrier to entry. Then we may see evolution of the mechanics, electronics and software for the robots start to accelerate I hope.
