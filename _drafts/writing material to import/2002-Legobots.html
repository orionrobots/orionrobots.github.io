Cad with Lego



Most of my models start life in two ways - either as jots on a scrap of paper - or a moment of inspired madness when I make a run for my lego and start building. The second can be a pain when I already have a model I really like and do want to scrap it.

Jotting is good for trying ideas and spur-o-the-moment designs, but you cannot take it very far. I am no picasso and sometimes I look at my scribblings later and wonder what it was I was trying to draw.

But a CAD application would be the best solution. For those of you new to all of this - that is Computer Aided Design. And even better would be mechanical simulation of the designs. There are two approaches with lego to this. One is to design your model first(which works sometimes), and the other is to input an old model before you pull it apart. I have made use of CAD for both. Incidentally- this is also a great way to introduce younger minds to CAD. ProE is extremely complex and anyone who wants to get into engineering would to well to be familiar with a basic LegoCad as a youngster. I had no such means so I had to rely on my general computer saviness to get by.

MLCad (http://www.lm-software.com/mlcad/) is the most useful Lego utility I have ever come across. Even more useful than the brick-seperator tool(sure beats using your teeth or fragile fingernails). If you have not got it I seriously suggest you go and downloaad it for free. I beleive Lego have a CAD system available to schools with Dacta at a very high price - but I have never used this.

If you are running Linux, you will want to try LeoCad. None of these yet have mechanical simulation, or indeed behavioural simulation, but being as you can download the sources may be someone could contribute this. They are not perfect yet, and lack many possible features, but its a very good start.

As I add content to the site, I will be adding files that can be used in MLCad and LeoCAD. It is worth noting that many Lego sites use this format.

Oh and just a note- if you are not running an OS which already has zip-archive support, you will probably need winzip as well. XP includes support, and most Linux distributions can handle these with "unzip" on the command line.


Me and Lego


Some of you may consider using Lego for anything serious is a little strange- after all it is a kids toy, isnt it?

When I was a kid, I certainly did have a large container full of the stuff, with which I built the usual imaginative round up of items - normally I only built models to the instructions once, and promptly lost the instructions.

There came a time, when I left home, to go to university. So I left my Lego and gave it to my sisters. Who promptly and rather methodically destroyed, defaced and lost most of it.

A few years later, I saw the RCX. This appeared to me to be the ULTIMATE lego item, so I bought it. And now I have bought many models since to supplement it including a number of ebay items - which I *always* remove from the box. I dont collect Lego for financial worth, or antique value, but so it can be used in modelling and robotics. Or even as you may put it - played with. After all- what is Lego if it is not played with.

Anyway after this I have decided I have not grown out of Lego, and most likely will never do so - I am not sure I really want to. I certainly wont be giving away or selling *any* of it ever again.

The RCX


At my university, I was studying AI(or Cognitive Sci as the course was titled later) as part of my joint honours. The department mostly ran neural net simulations on Apple Macs(I dont want to start any holy war here- but I despise Macs) and the occasional GA on a pc. If you want to know more- I may put up some info later.

But the department decided that after studying various robots we should be building and trying our own. Since we were more interested in behaviour and control than electronics - an RCX was a good answer. So a lot of information was covered and they looked likely to buy lego DACTA systems.

Sadly, it never happened while I was there, and AFAIK still has not. But it served well to whet my appetite for one of my own. I am competant with a soldering iron, and have fair experience with mechanical linkages, but lets face it Lego is just easier. If I want to build a quick prototype, and testbed a program - then the RCX is perfect. I quickly bought an extra motor and a few extras - and I have never regreted it.
